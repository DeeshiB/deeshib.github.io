Chapter 1

The Challenge: Teaching Under Constraint

Across the Global South, teachers in government schools work under extraordinary pressures: overcrowded classrooms, severe staffing shortages, multi-grade instruction, and heavy administrative mandates. In Karnataka, India, these conditions make lesson planning — a cornerstone of effective instruction — especially difficult.

Lesson plans serve a dual purpose: they are both a pedagogical tool for structuring instruction and a bureaucratic artifact required by education authorities. Teachers must prepare detailed documentation while simultaneously managing 40-60 students across multiple subjects and grade levels.

Pedagogical Need

Lesson plans help structure instruction, ensure curriculum coverage, and improve student outcomes — yet teachers lack the time to create them well.

Administrative Burden

Schools require detailed written documentation for compliance. Teachers spend significant time on record-keeping rather than teaching.

Language Barriers

Most AI tools are English-centric, creating gaps for teachers working in Kannada-medium classrooms where instruction happens in the local language.

Why existing AI tools fall short

Tools like Khanmigo and MagicSchool AI produce fluent lesson plans, but they often lack factual accuracy, cultural sensitivity, and contextual alignment — particularly for non-English, Global South contexts. LLMs can hallucinate, generate fabricated citations, and struggle to adapt content to local pedagogical norms.

This study examines Shiksha Copilot (Shiksha = education in Sanskrit/Hindi), a human-in-the-loop AI system designed to address these gaps through a structured workflow combining AI + Human Curation, Multilingual Support, and Teacher-AI Collaboration.

Chapter 2

Shiksha Copilot: Architecture & Design

Shiksha Copilot was developed in collaboration with an education partner organization that has worked in Karnataka's public schools for over 22 years. The system was built on five design principles:

📚

Curriculum Alignment

All lesson plans follow the state-mandated syllabus and grade-level learning objectives, grounded in actual textbook content via RAG.

🌐

Bilingual Support

Localized content for both English- and Kannada-medium classrooms using a fine-tuned translation model.

📱

Low-Tech Access

Mobile-first and offline-capable to accommodate infrastructure constraints in rural Karnataka.

🤝

Workflow Integration

Outputs aligned with administrative documentation standards, the 5E model, and classroom realities.

Technical Architecture

The system has three core components working together:

Curriculum Ingestion

Textbooks → OCR → Chunked & embedded in RAG index

→

LLM Orchestration

Agentic framework: RAG, LP Gen, Q&A, Chat, Validation

→

Translation

LoRA fine-tuned Sarvam-1 for English ↔ Kannada

Models Under the Hood

Shiksha Copilot is powered by a carefully chosen stack of AI models, each serving a distinct role in the pipeline:

Role Model Details
Core LLM & Orchestrator OpenAI GPT-4o Powers the agentic orchestration framework — drives lesson plan generation, question generation, lesson chat, edu chat, validation, and all RAG-augmented reasoning
Curriculum Extraction OpenAI GPT-4o Extracts Table of Contents metadata from textbook page images during the curriculum ingestion phase
Vector Embeddings OpenAI ada-embedding Embeds 512-token textbook chunks into vectors stored in Azure AI Search for retrieval-augmented generation
English ↔ Kannada Translation Sarvam-1 + LoRA A decoder-only Indic language LLM fine-tuned with Low-Rank Adaptation on education-domain bilingual data (textbook pairs, glossary, human-annotated examples)
Training Data Alignment OpenAI GPT-4 Used to create bilingual sentence pairs from English and Kannada textbooks for fine-tuning the translation model
OCR Tesseract + Azure Form Recognizer Extracts text from textbook PDFs; non-LLM components handling document digitization
Why GPT-4o as the backbone?

The entire agentic framework — including the RAG agent, Lesson Plan Generation agent, Question Generation agent, Lesson Chat, Edu Chat, and Validation agent — is driven by OpenAI's GPT-4o model. This choice reflects GPT-4o's strong multi-task reasoning capabilities. However, for translation, the team opted for a specialized approach: a LoRA-fine-tuned Sarvam-1 model pre-trained specifically for Indic languages, rather than relying on GPT-4o's general multilingual abilities. This decision acknowledges that general-purpose LLMs still underperform on low-resource languages compared to purpose-built models.

Why not just use ChatGPT or Meta AI directly?

Teachers in the study who had used ChatGPT or Meta AI before found them lacking in three key areas: (1) No curriculum grounding — teachers had to manually explain the lesson context each time. (2) Poor Kannada quality — general-purpose LLMs produced significantly worse content in Indian languages. (3) No human curation layer — outputs went directly to teachers without expert review. As Teacher 8 noted: "I can ask the same to Meta AI, but then I have to explain the lesson to Meta AI. Here I can ask directly." After using Shiksha Copilot, teachers' usage of both ChatGPT and Meta AI actually increased (never-used rates dropped from 43% to 11% for ChatGPT, 42% to 10% for Meta AI), suggesting the tool served as a gateway to broader AI literacy.

RAG-Powered Content Generation

Textbooks for Science, Math, Social Science, and English across grades 5–10 were extracted, chunked into 512-token segments, embedded using OpenAI's ada-embedding, and indexed in Azure AI Search. This ensures lesson plans are grounded in the actual curriculum rather than generic LLM knowledge.

Two-Phase Workflow

The system implements a carefully structured two-phase process that keeps humans in the loop at every critical juncture:

Phase 1: Curation

LLMs draft → 23 curators review, rate & refine

→

Translation

English LPs → Kannada via fine-tuned model

→

Phase 2: Distribution

Teachers access & customize curated plans

23 curators — 15 English-medium and 8 Kannada-medium educators — used the Curation Platform to develop base lesson plans.

Step 1: Learning Objectives

LLMs generate draft learning objectives, curators review and augment for comprehensive curricular coverage.

Step 2: LP Generation

RAG-enhanced LLMs generate full 5E-aligned lesson plans. Curators rate and revise each content block individually.

Step 3: Translation Review

Kannada-medium curators review machine translations for grammar, terminology, and stylistic consistency.

Teachers access curated plans through a mobile-friendly Distribution Platform and customize them for their classrooms.

Browse & Select

Teachers browse subject-wise, grade-wise curated lesson plans aligned to Karnataka's curriculum timeline.

Customize

Teachers copy plans and modify them — adjusting activities, examples, and assessments for their specific classroom context.

Use in Class

Plans serve both as teaching guides and administrative documentation, shared with school authorities.

Beyond lesson plans, Shiksha Copilot offered several supplementary AI features:

Lesson Chat

Curriculum-grounded chatbot for lesson-specific queries. Available to power users.

Edu Chat

General academic chatbot for broader educational queries. Available to power users.

Question Generator

Automated assessment creation using Bloom's Taxonomy. Teachers specify chapter coverage and cognitive depth.

AI-Assisted Editing

Power users could regenerate LP sections with teacher feedback, enabling iterative AI refinement.

With the system built, the researchers designed a rigorous mixed-methods study to understand how teachers actually used it…

Chapter 3

Research Design: A Mixed-Methods Study at Scale

The researchers conducted a large-scale mixed-methods study with 1,043 teachers and 23 curators across government schools in Karnataka, India, from December 2024 to March 2025. Three research questions guided the investigation:

RQ1: Teacher-AI Collaboration

How do teachers collaborate with AI in an LLM-based lesson planning system?

RQ2: Multilingual Challenges

What challenges and opportunities arise when implementing LLM-generated lesson plans in non-English classrooms?

RQ3: Work Practice Shifts

In what ways does an LLM-based lesson planning tool reshape the work practices of teachers?

Data Sources

Data Source Scope Purpose
Pre-survey 639 teachers Baseline on experience, attitudes, prior tech use
Post-survey 387 teachers Stress, time, pedagogy change, satisfaction
System logs 1,043 teachers Usage patterns, feature adoption, session behavior
Teacher interviews 32 teachers In-depth qualitative insights on collaboration and adaptation
Curator interviews 10 curators Curation workflows, AI content quality assessment
LP quality evaluation 160 lesson plans 3 expert raters evaluated accuracy, pedagogy, contextualization

Teacher Demographics

71% female. Median experience: 11–20 years. 45% teaching 4+ subjects. 67% teaching 3+ grade levels simultaneously.

Medium Split

660 English-medium teachers and 383 Kannada-medium teachers, reflecting Karnataka's bilingual school system.

Ethical safeguards

The study received IRB approval. Teachers were informed of data collection through information sheets, with explicit consent. All interviews were conducted in Kannada/English per teacher preference. No personally identifiable data was included in analysis.

The data revealed a rich picture of how teachers actually engaged with AI-generated content — and where the system fell short…

The Kannada quality gap

Nearly half (47%) of Kannada content blocks needed major edits, compared to just 6% for English. Statistical details: Inter-rater reliability

Inter-rater reliability was assessed using Krippendorff's alpha. For English LPs, alpha ranged from 0.74 (engagement) to 0.86 (accuracy), indicating substantial agreement. For Kannada LPs, alpha ranged from 0.62 to 0.79. English LPs scored significantly higher across all dimensions (p < .001, Mann-Whitney U tests).

The Multilingual Reality

A critical finding was the stark quality difference between English and Kannada lesson plans. While English LPs were largely reliable, Kannada content faced persistent challenges:

Why this matters: the "low-resource language" challenge

Kannada has over 44 million speakers, yet remains "low-resource" in the context of LLMs due to limited training data, fewer NLP benchmarks, and insufficient representation in multilingual models. This study provides concrete evidence that current LLMs produce significantly lower quality educational content in such languages, necessitating robust human-in-the-loop mechanisms.

Contextualization: Promise and Limits

Teachers valued how Shiksha Copilot incorporated state-level contextual references — examples from Karnataka's geography, culture, and local industries. However, teachers identified a gap between state-level and hyper-local contextualization:

What worked

State-level references (Karnataka geography, local crops, regional festivals) were seen as useful starting points for instruction.

What was missing

Hyper-local references tied to specific neighborhoods, communities, and students' lived experiences. Teachers filled these gaps using their own expertise.

Beyond content quality, Shiksha Copilot fundamentally reshaped how teachers work — for better and for worse…

The 5E-structured lesson plans naturally encouraged more interactive instruction. Teachers reported that the Engage and Elaborate sections were particularly influential, providing concrete activity ideas they might not have developed on their own.

Teachers as critical evaluators, not passive consumers

Teachers did not simply adopt AI content wholesale. They actively evaluated, adapted, and sometimes rejected AI suggestions. 42% customized lesson plans, and many more made mental adaptations during classroom delivery. This "selective appropriation" underscores that effective AI integration depends on teacher agency, not just tool quality.

Administrative Relief & Organizational Dynamics

Shiksha Copilot eased a significant administrative burden. Teachers used the tool primarily for documentation compliance, with several interesting organizational effects:

📋

Documentation as primary driver

Many teachers adopted Shiksha Copilot initially for administrative record-keeping rather than pedagogical improvement, reflecting the reality that documentation demands drive tool adoption in resource-constrained settings.

👥

Peer-mediated adoption

WhatsApp groups and staff-room conversations became critical channels for adoption. Teachers shared tips, troubleshooted issues, and validated AI content through informal peer networks, forming organic communities of practice.

⚠️

Headmaster gatekeeping

School administrators played a pivotal role: supportive headmasters encouraged adoption and validated AI-generated plans, while resistant ones created barriers. Some even requested Shiksha Copilot access themselves.

🚧

Systemic constraints persist

Despite time savings, fundamental challenges — staffing shortages, multi-grade teaching, infrastructure gaps — limited the degree of pedagogical transformation. AI tools can reduce friction but cannot resolve structural inequities.

Feature Adoption: Beyond Lesson Plans

Teachers creatively appropriated features beyond their intended use, demonstrating adaptive agency:

Question Paper Generator

Originally designed for formative assessment, teachers used it to prepare question papers for exams, the most popular supplementary feature. They valued the Bloom's Taxonomy alignment for creating cognitively varied assessments.

Lesson Chat as Study Aid

Some teachers used the curriculum-grounded chatbot to learn unfamiliar content themselves before teaching it — particularly common among multi-subject teachers assigned outside their expertise.

"When I have to teach a topic in Science that I'm not confident about, I first use the Lesson Chat to understand it myself. Then I teach it to my students."
— Teacher assigned to teach outside their specialty

These findings point toward a broader vision for how AI tools should be designed for education in the Global South…

Chapter 6

Design Directions & Implications for the Future

The study offers three key contributions to CSCW and HCI research, alongside concrete design principles for future teacher-centered EdTech:

🛠

System Contribution

A working human-in-the-loop AI system for bilingual lesson planning, demonstrating how to build Human-AI systems for linguistically diverse educational contexts.

🔬

Empirical Evidence

Large-scale evidence of how teachers evaluate, adapt, and contextualize AI content — revealing both the strengths and limits of current LLMs.

🌎

Workflow Transformation

Evidence of how AI tools reshape teaching work in Global South classrooms, influencing instructional practices, administrative processes, and organizational dynamics.

Design Principles for Teacher-Centered AI

🤝

Keep teachers as decision-makers

AI should augment, not replace, teacher expertise. Treat teachers as co-agents with bounded, negotiable AI roles. Design for "selective appropriation" — teachers choosing what to adopt, adapt, or reject.

🌐

Invest in low-resource languages

Current LLMs produce significantly lower quality content in languages like Kannada. Solutions include community-driven data collection, fine-tuned translation models, and robust human review for non-English outputs.

👥

Embed AI within communities of practice

AI adoption in schools is social, not individual. Design for peer-mediated learning, WhatsApp-based support, and mentor networks. Leverage existing communities rather than creating isolated tool experiences.

📈

Design for both pedagogy and compliance

In low-resource schools, administrative documentation is not a secondary concern — it often drives tool adoption. Design systems that serve dual purposes: improving instruction while easing bureaucratic workload.

🌱

Enable hyper-local contextualization

Move beyond state-level or national content to support neighborhood-level examples. Consider community-sourced content libraries and teacher-contributed local knowledge bases.

⚖️

Address structural constraints honestly

AI tools can ease friction but cannot solve systemic problems like staffing shortages, infrastructure gaps, or top-down administrative mandates. Design with awareness of what technology can and cannot change.

A cautionary note on deskilling

The authors caution that heavy reliance on AI-generated lesson plans could potentially deskill teachers over time, reducing their ability to independently design instruction. The human-in-the-loop curation model and emphasis on teacher customization are deliberate design choices to mitigate this risk. Future longitudinal studies should monitor whether pedagogical planning skills are maintained.

Limitations & Open Questions

Short deployment window

The 4-month deployment (Dec 2024 – Mar 2025) limits conclusions about sustained adoption and long-term pedagogical change. Novelty effects may inflate early engagement.

No student outcome data

The study measured teacher adoption and workflow changes but did not assess whether students actually learned more or better as a result.

Selection bias

Participating teachers were supported by a partner organization with 22 years of presence. Results may not generalize to schools without such institutional support.

Single state context

Karnataka has specific curricula, policies, and language dynamics. Generalizability to other Indian states or Global South contexts requires further study.

The bigger picture

This study demonstrates that AI-assisted lesson planning can meaningfully support teachers in low-resource schools — not by replacing their expertise, but by providing a scaffolded starting point they can shape with their own knowledge. The key insight is that effective Teacher-AI Collaboration requires treating teachers as partners, not users; investing in local languages and contexts; and embedding AI tools within the social and institutional fabric of schools.
